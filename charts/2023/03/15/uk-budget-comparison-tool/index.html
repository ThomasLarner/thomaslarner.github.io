Today’s budget should be a breath of fresh air for everyone. Unlike the seven most recent major policy changes made over the last three years, there was no nuclear, economic fallout after, or any major economic crisis as a backdrop to the announcement (discounting inflation that seems to have peaked).

Thankfully, Jeremy Hunt’s emergency measures from November seem to have worked, so that now he can switch his focus from stability to the growth mandate on which they were elected, albeit in a more sensible way.

The reaction to this budget was one of “Great!.. Well anyways…”. The yield on a 10 year Gilt basically remained the same throughout – a welcome nod of approval from the lenders and bondholders.

I’d say that this budget was a reassuring and sensible approach to growth in the long term, whilst having a crack at reducing the government’s whopping debt burden. It should help to reinforce that the country is in safe hands, and to rebuild trust in government policy making.

Information on the chart above:

Along the x-axis is changes to the Debt Management Office’s financing remit, i.e. how many bonds the DMO need to sell to finance the government’s fiscal policy. Along the y-axis is the effects of tax changes on The Exchequer over 5 years, i.e. how much money The Exchequer earns or loses from their tax changes over 5 years. The further the point it away from the origin – the more extreme the budget. Hover over each point to see when each change was made.
