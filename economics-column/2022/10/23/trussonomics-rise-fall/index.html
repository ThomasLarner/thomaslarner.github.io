Trussonomics: The Rise and Fall

How Trussonomics fell apart after just one announcement.

Oct 23, 2022

When Liz Truss campaigned for Tory party leader, she set out a vision for Britain that promised growth, fuelled by low taxes and deregulation, breaking away from “Treasury orthodoxy” and carrying out almost Thatcherite-like reforms. Branded “Trussonomics”, this was supposed to be the path that solved many of Britain’s underlying economic problems. However, after only three weeks into her tenure, it became apparent it was all a fairy-tale after all, just as her campaign rival, and replacement Prime Minister, Rishi Sunak had predicted.

The Rise

Over the last decade, Britain has jumped many economic hurdles: Brexit, at least so far, has created more red tape for businesses, hampering exports and trade; the COVID crisis, which has raised unemployment and inactivity in the workforce; and the recent cost of living crisis and supply side constraints threaten to plunge the UK into recession.

So, what is going on?

Well, as Liz Truss would say, Britain’s underlying economic failures have been a by-product of low growth, and inactivity from the establishment, who accept the UK is facing an inevitable economic decline.

So when she had the chance to stand for leader of the Conservative party, after Boris’ resignation, she set out her radical plan to lead Britain back to high growth. This, she said, would cure the UK’s economy, and importantly help the UK mitigate a near-term recession.

I was critical of her plans to cut taxes during a period of historically high inflation. Suggesting that this would only fuel more spending and inflation, prompting the Bank of England to raise rates further, not only harming people with mortgages but also the government's ability to borrow cheaply. This, I believed, would plunge the UK into recession.

However, I do agree with her supply side reforms, which compliment the BoE’s cooling inflation agenda and would also promote long-term growth in the future. I think borrowing money in order to carry out these reforms is also perfectly acceptable as they will help to generate more income through higher GDP down the line.

The Fall

The fall of Trussonomics came at the very beginning. The start of her radical, pro-growth plans were unveiled at her not-so-mini-mini-budget, where it quickly became apparent that growth sounds all well and good, until you actually start doing it.

The immediate fallout were drastic falls in GBP, and a sell off of gilts. Later on, the Bank of England had to reverse any of it’s hopes of QT, and started buying gilts to try to supress yields. This all suggested that investors and markets did not like Truss’ budget. Remember, that it is these very investors who Truss needs to borrow from in order to carry out her expensive reforms.

It is important to note, and perhaps this is something that has been missing from many media reports, is that the reason pension funds were having to fire sell gilts was not necessarily because they had lost confidence in the government, but rather to meet margin calls. I think it would have been an easy oversight for Truss and Kwarteng to miss the fact that pension funds were jacked up with leverage, due to their complex hedging strategies. Even Jamie Dimon, CEO of J.P.Morgan, said he was surprised at the sheer amount of leverage in the pension industry. This is something that has been overlooked in the media, but also not something to distract from the fundamentally flawed budget.

In hindsight, it was a bad idea to announce all her policies in one go. Her tax cuts, which totalled £146bn over 5 years, and her borrowing plans that were at levels similar to budgets during the 2008 financial crisis were simply too much for the markets to swallow in one gulp.

U-turns were therefore inevitable, despite the fact Truss should have stuck to her guns, she sacked her chancellor and then reversed almost her entire budget with her new chancellor Jeremy Hunt.

At that point, resignation was a matter of when, not if.

She resigned after only 44 days in office. Such was the epic downfall of Trussonomics.
