We Mustn’t Be Too Bullish On China’s Reopening

An increasingly disjointed global economy means the US may not benefit from it.

Mar 19, 2023

S peculation over China’s reopening dominated China-talk last year, with everyone patiently waiting for China to come bursting out of its gates and to unleash its economy on the world.

Goldman Sachs, for one, have identified 3 main “channels” through which China will help to boost global growth in 2023, in a report released in February. They expect China’s increased domestic demand, international travel and commodity demand to help raise global GDP by roughly 1% in 2023.

However, amid heightened tensions between the United States and China; lacking confidence in the country and their increasing turn to cheap Russian commodities, we must be careful not to assume these channels will lead to USD.

“Revenge spending”

From inside the country, there was talk of pent-up demand that was bursting at the seams and of consumers that were eager to spend their colossal pandemic savings. The average Chinese household saved over a third of their income last year, compare that to American’s 3.3% last year and only 17% during the locked-down year of 2020.

In February, however, retail sales disappointed and were down slightly by 0.02% MoM. Gasoline prices popped by 7% on a month-to-month basis, suggesting that the people are getting out, but may be hesitating about parting with their cash – highlighting the low consumer confidence in the wake of zero-Covid.

Once confidence does return, I still think much of their spending will be done on goods made by firms based in Asia. After all, China is “the world’s factory” and produce most of their own goods; of what they do import, significantly more comes from Asia than from western countries.

It is also possible that these savings will be put aside for a rainy day. Since the removal of zero-Covid policies, the government has refined the definition of Covid – making it harder for patients to claim on their health insurance for Covid treatment. Some hospitals have been reportedly charging thousands of dollars per day for treatment, which during the days of zero-Covid would have been state supported in many provinces. Many people may continue to build up savings in case Covid cases start to rise again.

International holidays

Now that quarantine rules for returning travellers to the mainland have been scrapped, there has been speculation that there will be an increase in international travel from China in 2023. Thailand, for one, is expecting somewhere between 7 and 10 million travellers from China this year, which is still slightly down from pre-pandemic levels, but should still provide a nice little boost to Thailand’s GDP.

However, visa data from the United States suggests that travellers were initially, at least, slow to apply for tourism visas. In January, there were 13,300 tourism visa applications from China, compared to 54,000 in January 2020. Considering that it is recommended you apply for a visa three months in advance of travelling, the overall picture from the data released so far suggests there will be a slow pick-up in demand for travelling to the US in 2023. This can be put down to a couple of factors.

Firstly, US-China tensions have created a stalemate over flight permits to and from the countries. Chinese airlines have submitted requests for ramping up flights to the US, but sources suggest that the Department of Transport is unlikely to approve these until their Chinese counterparts similarly approve US airline requests.

Secondly, there is still uncertainty over travel restrictions. Whilst quarantine rules have been scrapped, Malaysia’s AirAsia X airline are reportedly still struggling to sell tickets to Chinese travellers, reporting that they can only fill half of their available seats, owing to a lack of uncertainty over the rules if you were to test positive on your return.

No doubt travel will eventually rebound, just like it has for countries who ditched covid rules long ago, but we won’t see a major rebound until Q3/4, accounting for the three month lag and the time it takes for travellers to gain more confidence that there will be no sudden changes to the restrictions.

Commodity prices

The third channel that Goldman Sachs identifies is a rise in demand for commodities, such as oil, as production lines fire up in the country. There will certainly be an increase in demand for oil, as mentioned already, gasoline prices have been rising – suggesting an underlying increase in demand for petrol and diesel, which is a result of removing lockdown restrictions.

There are two sides to this: Commodity exporting nations should benefit from extra demand which would translate into higher prices and profits; whilst, net importers of commodities would pay the price.

However, China is increasingly turning to Russian crude oil, due to its low price – a product of western sanctions. According to Refinitiv, China increased its imports of Russian crude from 1.52 million bpd in December to 2.03 million bpd in January. Considering that the Goldman report says they expect a 1 million bpd increase in aggregate Chinese demand for crude over the whole of 2023, it would seem that Russia has already fulfilled half of this already. With reports that China is also settling oil contracts in Rubles and Yuan, this further moves capital away from USD.

Russia’s disconnectedness from the global oil market, a result of sanctions, means that it is tricky to predict how Chinese demand for oil will affect the global price of oil. In OPEC’s meeting in October, they agreed to cut production by 2 million bpd for the whole of 2023, suggesting that they are taking the “wait and see” approach too. Demand from the rest of the world may slow this year, due to the economic climate in the west, which might make room for extra Chinese demand within the current supply.

If oil demand does rise significantly, then net importers of oil will face higher import costs. This will likely stoke inflation higher, perhaps prompting more monetary policy action in many western countries. But much of China’s demand for oil may depend on demand for its goods by these western nations, who are already hit with inflation and slowing economic conditions.

Wait and see

There is immense uncertainty surrounding China’s reopening. Yes, over the one to two year horizon, demand for travel, commodities and domestic demand will recover, and China will probably help to boost global GDP in 2023. However, the China-bulls are wrong to expect a flick-of-the-switch return to pre-pandemic conditions. China’s modest 5% growth target indicates the wariness of Chinese officials about the level of confidence in the economy at present – a sign that we should be wary too.

We must also be careful that in this increasingly disjointed version of globalisation that we find ourselves in, not to assume China’s reopening will help western countries as much as itself, its BRICS allies and its regional trade partners, who will benefit from closer diplomatic ties with China. A stark contrast to the United States’ current foreign policy.
